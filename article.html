Europe

The Map That Kept Changing

Europe's press freedom story is not a straight line. Between 2015 and 2025, some countries gained ground, others lost it quietly, and the direction of change did not follow the map most readers would draw in their heads.

Source: World Bank Data360 / RSF • Time: 2015 – 2025

Press freedom tends to make headlines through moments of crisis: a newsroom raided, a journalist detained, a law passed overnight. But a decade of RSF index scores tells a slower story: not a single continental trend, but a gradual pulling apart.

The sharpest fall belongs to Russia, which dropped more than 30 points over ten years: a decline so large it sets the scale for everything else. But Russia is not the surprise. The surprise is what happened elsewhere. Montenegro and North Macedonia, two Balkan countries outside Europe's usual high-scoring press-freedom cluster, posted the largest improvements in the dataset. Neither reached the top of the index, but both moved upward.

So did the quiet slippage in Western Europe. Austria lost more than 11 points over the decade. Belgium dropped nearly 8, Finland more than 5, while Denmark and Germany also slipped. These are countries that still score well in absolute terms, but they were not standing still.

Direction of Change

A country can score low in 2025 while moving upward, or remain high while slipping. Russia is the outlier; the more revealing pattern is that movement also appears among countries that still sit far higher on the index.

Caveat

Comparative index

The RSF score combines political, legal, economic, sociocultural and safety dimensions. It cannot explain why a score moved or show variation within each media system. Because RSF revised its methodology in 2022, read the decade comparison as broad movement rather than exact measurement.
