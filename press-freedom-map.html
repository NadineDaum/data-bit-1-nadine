MEDIA FREEDOM

The Map That Kept Changing

Europe’s press freedom story is not a straight line. Between 2015 and 2025, some countries gained ground, others lost it quietly, and the direction of change did not follow the map many readers might expect.

Source: World Bank Data360 / RSF • Time: 2015 – 2025

Biggest gains

    Press freedom usually becomes visible in moments of rupture: a newsroom searched, a journalist detained, a law passed in the middle of a political fight. But index scores tell a slower story. Over a decade, Europe did not move as one. It pulled apart.

    The steepest fall belongs to Russia, which dropped more than 30 points over ten years. The scale of that decline is so large that it can make the rest of the map look secondary. But Russia is not the only story here, and in some ways it is not the most surprising one.

    Montenegro and North Macedonia, two Balkan countries outside Europe’s usual high-scoring press-freedom cluster, posted the largest improvements in the dataset. Neither reached the top of the index. But both moved upward, a reminder that starting point and direction are not the same thing.

    The same is true at the other end of the map. Some of Western Europe’s strongest media systems slipped without falling into obvious crisis. Austria lost more than 11 points over the decade. Belgium dropped nearly 8, Finland more than 5, while Denmark and Germany also moved downward. These countries still score well in absolute terms. But high scores can make gradual pressure harder to see.

    Direction of Change

    Caveat

    Comparative index

    The RSF score combines political, legal, economic, sociocultural and safety dimensions. It cannot explain why a score moved or show variation within each media system. Because RSF revised its methodology in 2022, read the decade comparison as broad movement.
